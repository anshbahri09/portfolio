Where does India stand after 66 years of independence

August 15, 2013 by Ansh Bahri



August 15, 1947 marked the end of the British rule on the Indian Subcontinent. There were numerous peaks and valleys for India to cross over after the long and painful freedom struggle, sacrificing hundreds of lives to achieve what is locally called as purna swaraj meaning total independence. Some people believe that the British rule over India had helped it in its development, leaving behind them a functional system for the government, facilities like Railways n a lot more which India on its own might not have ever been able to achieve. Others believe that the Britishers are responsible for fuelling the differences between Hindus and Muslims as the end of the colonial rule of the Indian Subcontinent partitioned it into two states of India and Pakistan.


Whatever be it, back in India school children in cities like Chandigarh, Bangalore dress up in traditional attire to perform during the independence day function. The Indian Prime Minister Dr. Manmohan Singh along with many more ministers addresses the nation from the ramparts of Red Fort in New Delhi and salute to the Indian Flag to pledge their allegiance to the service of the county. The day is celebrated like none other, by people of all religion and in all parts of the country.


It is a shame when all the newspapers and news channels have to talk about is about the firing which continues at the Ind-Pak border even on the day of their independence. The aftereffects of million dollar scams that we hear of, on almost a monthly basis has led the growth down the drain and inflation on the rise. Even though being the worlds largest democracy and fearless of the competition from across the seas, the internal security challenges are compounded by unsettled international borders. As India tries to increase its grasp over science and technology with the moon mission, the talks with China seem to be never ending. The value of rupee to dollar keeps falling. Looks like a very hazy road where the representatives do not represent the citizens, but their own needs.
